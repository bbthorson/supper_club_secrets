# Supper Club Secrets — Series Plan (Books 1–6)

## Series Overview

Six friends along Brooklyn's G line solve neighborhood mysteries over weekly dinner parties. Each of the first six books centers on one character, peeling back their layers while the group's reputation quietly grows from "that was a weird coincidence" to "those are the people who fix things."

**Structure:** Every book follows a four-meal format over the course of one month. Meals map loosely to Save the Cat beats: Setup → Investigation → Crisis → Resolution.

**Setting:** Brooklyn and Queens, loosely along the G line — Williamsburg, Greenpoint, Bushwick, Carroll Gardens, Park Slope, Fort Greene, and potentially into Astoria.

**Tone Dial:** 1–10 scale. Most scenes sit at 3–5. Crisis peaks at 6–7. Never exceeds 7 across the series.

**POV:** Emma remains primary POV throughout all six books (she observes through food, kitchen metaphors, and her particular blind spots).

## Book 1: The Case of the Missing Hot Sauce (COMPLETE)

**Focal Character:** Emma Hartley
**Month:** October
**Host:** Emma (all four meals)
**Status:** Expanded draft complete (~33,400 words, 18 chapters)

**Mystery:** Hank, a beloved hot sauce vendor, vanishes from the McGolrick Park farmers market. The group discovers a corporate land grab backed by a mogul using shell companies and legal intimidation — including weaponizing Emma's secret OnlyFans account.

**Character Arc:** Emma moves from defensive secrecy to openness. Her instincts about "Big Hot Sauce" turn out to be right. She learns to trust the group and herself.

**Secret Addressed:** Kinky Kitchen (Emma's OnlyFans) — revealed to the group in Ch. 13, goes public by Ch. 18.

**Reputation Status:** The group doesn't think of themselves as mystery-solvers yet. This was a one-time thing. Right?

**Key Minor Characters Introduced:** Hank, Dorothy, Sofia, Marcus, Brenda Marquez

## Book 2: [Working Title TBD]

**Focal Character:** Oliver
**Month:** November
**Host:** Olivia & Oliver (their Williamsburg one-bedroom)

**Mystery Concept:**
A series of small, beloved businesses along the G line corridor are being forced out — not through obvious gentrification, but through a quiet, bureaucratic squeeze. Permits delayed indefinitely. Landmark protections mysteriously withdrawn. Building inspections that always find just enough violations to shut a place down for weeks.

Oliver notices the pattern first — not through the supper club, but through his secret online life. His pseudonymous zoning-violations Twitter account has been tracking anomalies for weeks. One of his subreddit contacts flags a connection: all the targeted businesses sit on parcels that were recently rezoned under a little-noticed amendment. Someone is gaming the city's administrative machinery to clear blocks for development without ever having to buy anyone out.

The mystery stumbles into the group's lap when Sofia (from Book 1) mentions that her cheese shop just failed an inspection for the first time in twenty years — for a code violation she's never heard of.

**Why Oliver:**
- The mystery lives in his professional wheelhouse (zoning, permits, municipal records)
- His secret online life becomes essential to the investigation — and threatens to be exposed
- His relationship with Olivia gets tested when the investigation requires him to operate independently, publicly, and confidently
- The canon note about "an awkward encounter with Jasper at a city office" pays off here

**Character Arc:**
Oliver must step out from behind his screens — both the anonymous ones and the ones Olivia provides. When his pseudonymous posts get dangerously close to identifying the scheme, he faces a choice: protect his anonymous comfort zone or go public with what he knows. The group sees the confident, articulate person his 3,000 Twitter followers already know.

**Secret Progression:** Oliver's online life edges toward exposure. By the end, at least one person in the group (probably Jasper, who stumbles across Oliver's account during the city office encounter) knows or strongly suspects. Oliver doesn't fully confess, but the door is cracked open.

**Thanksgiving Beat:** The November setting puts Thanksgiving in play. Meal 3 or 4 could fall on or near the holiday — forcing the group to balance investigation with family obligations. Oliver's quiet New Jersey parents could visit, giving us a window into who he was before Olivia. Elijah's family obligations pull him briefly off-screen (seeding Book 3).

**Seasonal Texture:** Late autumn. Bare trees, early dark, the particular cold that comes off the East River. First real coat weather. Thanksgiving grocery chaos at the market. The neighborhood shifting from fall charm to winter endurance.

**Minor Character Opportunities:**
- Sofia returns (her shop is threatened)
- Marcus at The Gilded Fern may also be affected (different block, same pattern)
- New: A city clerk or inspector who's been pressured to rubber-stamp violations
- New: A longtime tenant organizer or community board member who's been fighting this quietly

**Reputation Status:** Still feels accidental. "We helped Hank, and now Sofia needs help too." But someone outside the group — maybe the tenant organizer — says something like "I heard you all handled that hot sauce situation."

## Book 3: [Working Title TBD]

**Focal Character:** Elijah Miller
**Month:** December
**Host:** Emma (comfort food season — her kitchen is where the group goes to feel warm)

**Mystery Concept:**
A longtime neighborhood institution — a community credit union that's served working-class and immigrant families in the G line corridor for decades — is on the verge of collapse. Members are getting letters that their accounts are being "restructured." Small business loans are being called in early. The board has been quietly replaced by outsiders.

Elijah recognizes the pattern immediately. It's asset-stripping — the same playbook private equity uses on vulnerable companies, applied to a community institution. Someone is hollowing out the credit union from the inside, planning to collapse it and sell the real estate (a beautiful old building on a suddenly valuable corner).

The mystery reaches the group when Emma mentions that Dorothy (the honey vendor from Book 1) came to the market in tears because the credit union is calling in a small business loan she's had for fifteen years, and she might lose her stall.

**Why Elijah:**
- The financial mechanics are his native language — he sees what no one else can
- The investigation forces him to use his banking knowledge in ways his employer wouldn't approve of
- December's holiday obligations pull him toward family — his mother's calls, his sister needing money for car repairs (seeded in Book 1), the expectation that he'll come home for Christmas
- The credit union's mission (serving families who can't access traditional banking) mirrors what his own family needed and never had

**Character Arc:**
Elijah has spent his career navigating a financial system that wasn't built for people like him. This investigation forces him to use those skills not for survival or for a paycheck, but for his community. It's the first time he's risked something professionally — accessing information he shouldn't, making calls that could get him fired — for something that matters to him personally. His dream of the upstate family compound gets seeded when he sees what it means for a community to lose its financial anchor.

**Secret Progression:** The family compound dream doesn't surface directly, but the emotional foundation is laid. We see Elijah quietly researching real estate upstate on his phone during a quiet moment. Emma notices but doesn't ask.

**December Texture:** Holiday pressure. Elijah's family expects him home. The group navigates December schedules — office parties, travel plans, shorter days. The credit union investigation has a deadline: the board vote to approve the "restructuring" is set for January 2nd, giving them exactly one month. Christmas decorations go up around the neighborhood while the credit union members' lives fall apart. The contrast between holiday warmth and institutional cold is the tonal engine.

**Minor Character Opportunities:**
- Dorothy returns (her loan is threatened — carries forward from Book 1)
- New: The credit union's original founder's granddaughter, who still works as a teller and suspects something is wrong but doesn't have the vocabulary to name it
- New: A retired accountant or bookkeeper from the neighborhood who kept the old records
- Brenda Marquez (from Book 1) could surface tangentially — maybe the real estate angle connects to her world

**Reputation Status:** The Dorothy connection makes this feel organic — "we helped her friend Hank, now she's coming to us." But it's the first time someone outside the friend group has specifically sought them out. The crew doesn't name it yet, but the pattern is forming.

## Book 4: [Working Title TBD]

**Focal Character:** Olivia
**Month:** January
**Host:** Rotating — each meal at a different home (Olivia's "new year, new energy" idea)
- Meal 1: Emma's (familiar ground)
- Meal 2: Noah's (minimalist discomfort, comedy)
- Meal 3: Jasper's (chaotic warmth, vinyl on the turntable)
- Meal 4: Olivia & Oliver's (coming home, resolution)

**Mystery Concept:**
One of Olivia's many past business contacts — a woman she "collected" during her pop-up vintage sales phase — is accused of running an elaborate art fraud scheme through a small gallery in Carroll Gardens. Fake provenance documents, forged authentication letters, stolen pieces laundered through estate sales. The accusation comes from a rival gallerist, and the evidence looks damning.

But Olivia doesn't believe it. She knew this woman. She liked this woman. And the accusation doesn't match the person she remembers — or does it? The investigation forces Olivia to confront the difference between knowing everyone and truly knowing anyone.

**Why Olivia:**
- The mystery lives in her world — networking, local business, curated taste
- Her superpower (reading people, collecting contacts) becomes both asset and liability
- January's post-holiday emptiness amplifies her restlessness — she's between projects again, and the investigation fills the void
- Her relationship with Oliver gets complicated when Oliver operates confidently and independently during the investigation (building on his Book 2 growth), and she doesn't know whether to be proud or threatened

**Character Arc:**
Olivia has always been the one who connects people, who sees the best in everyone, who collects relationships like curios. This mystery forces her to reckon with the cost of surface-level connection. Did she actually know this woman, or did she just like the idea of knowing her? The investigation also confronts her with a mirror: the accused woman is, in many ways, another version of Olivia — charming, networked, always moving to the next thing. The difference is one of them got caught.

**Secret Progression:** Her anxiety about Oliver's dependence surfaces when, for the first time, Oliver doesn't need her to translate or advocate for him. He's comfortable, he's contributing independently, and Olivia feels — unexpectedly — lost. She doesn't voice it, but Emma notices something is off.

**Rotating Homes — World-Building Opportunities:**
- **Meal 1 at Emma's:** Comfort, familiarity. The group is back where it started. January cold outside, warm kitchen inside.
- **Meal 2 at Noah's:** Comedy of minimalism. Not enough chairs. No wine glasses (he uses geometric tumblers). Air purifier too loud. But the East River view at night is stunning, and Noah is surprisingly proud of his space.
- **Meal 3 at Jasper's:** The Bushwick apartment is a revelation. Books everywhere, vinyl organized obsessively (contradicting the chaos), mismatched but comfortable. Jasper is a natural, relaxed host — the opposite of what everyone expected. Seeds of "who is this guy really?" planted for Book 6.
- **Meal 4 at Olivia & Oliver's:** Their space tells their story — her warmth, his order. The resolution dinner here feels like Olivia reclaiming her home and her sense of self.

**January Texture:** Post-holiday quiet. The neighborhood feels smaller in winter — fewer people on the streets, shops closing early, the G train even less reliable than usual. Carroll Gardens in January: brownstone stoops dusted with salt, café windows fogged from the inside. The art gallery world as a pocket of warmth and pretension against the cold.

**Minor Character Opportunities:**
- New: The accused gallerist (Olivia's former contact) — sympathetic, complicated
- New: The rival gallerist who made the accusation — not a simple villain
- Marcus surfaces (The Gilded Fern carries some of the same artists)
- New: An estate sale regular who notices patterns in what's being sold

**Reputation Status:** The accused woman reaches out to Olivia specifically because she's "heard about what you and your friends did for that hot sauce guy and the credit union thing." This is the first time the group's track record is explicitly cited by a stranger. Olivia is flattered. Noah is uncomfortable. Jasper is delighted.

## Book 5: [Working Title TBD]

**Focal Character:** Noah
**Month:** February
**Host:** Jasper (his Bushwick apartment — after Book 4 revealed it's actually a great space)

**Mystery Concept:**
A hyper-local neighborhood app — something like a Nextdoor competitor specifically for the G line corridor — has become wildly popular over the past few months. It helps people find babysitters, report broken streetlights, share restaurant recommendations. Everyone loves it. Noah championed it to the group.

Then strange things start happening. A local business gets review-bombed through the app after refusing to pay for a "premium listing." A tenant organizing meeting gets doxxed. A family's home address is leaked after they complained about a landlord. The app isn't just connecting the neighborhood — it's being weaponized.

Noah initially defends the platform (it's a technology problem with a technology solution). But as the investigation deepens, he discovers the app's data practices are predatory, its moderation is outsourced to people who don't live in the community, and the real business model is selling hyper-local behavioral data to real estate developers. The tech that was supposed to help the neighborhood is the tool being used to reshape it.

**Why Noah:**
- The mystery lives in his world — tech, platforms, digital infrastructure
- His core belief (technology solves problems) is directly challenged
- The investigation requires analog, human-intelligence methods he's always dismissed
- February + Valentine's Day creates comedy around his minimalist, anti-sentiment persona

**Character Arc:**
Noah has to confront the possibility that his worldview is not just incomplete but actively harmful. He championed this app. He told people to use it. The damage it's doing is, in part, his responsibility — a softer echo of Jasper's Book 1 mistake, which deepens their still-healing relationship. His arc isn't about abandoning tech, but about understanding that technology without community accountability is just another extraction tool.

**Secret Progression:** The sneaker collection gets exposed. The investigation leads to Noah's apartment (maybe the app's data reveals something about his building), and during a group scene, someone opens the wrong closet. The reveal is played for warmth, not humiliation — the group is surprised, then delighted. Elijah: "You own forty thousand dollars in shoes and you tried to serve us sulfur-free wine." Noah's hypocrisy becomes the thing that makes him most human.

**Valentine's Day Beat:** Noah is aggressively indifferent to Valentine's Day. The group finds this hilarious. Emma makes heart-shaped something as a joke. But the holiday gives the book an emotional undercurrent about what we value, what we collect, what we hold onto — connecting Noah's sneakers to the app's promise of "community" to the question of whether connection can be manufactured.

**February Texture:** Deep winter. The G line corridor at its most intimate — everyone bundled, moving fast, ducking into warm spaces. Jasper's Bushwick apartment becomes a refuge. Steamed-up windows. The particular silence of snow on Brooklyn streets (when it comes). Valentine's decorations in bodega windows.

**Minor Character Opportunities:**
- Sofia returns (her cheese shop was review-bombed on the app)
- New: The app's creator — a well-meaning developer who lost control of what they built
- New: A teenage coder from the neighborhood who figures out the data pipeline before Noah does

**Reputation Status:** The group is now sought out. The teenage coder finds them specifically. The app developer knows about them. Someone on the app has created a thread titled "who are those dinner party people who keep solving stuff?" Jasper screenshots it and frames it.

## Book 6: [Working Title TBD]

**Focal Character:** Jasper
**Month:** March
**Host:** Elijah (his apartment — the group finally enters his world)

**Mystery Concept:**
A series of high-value items — antique furniture, rare books, a small but significant art collection — are being quietly donated to Brooklyn cultural institutions. Anonymous gifts, no strings attached, worth a small fortune collectively. It seems benevolent until the pattern reveals something darker: every item traces back to estates that were contested, families that were pressured, and inheritances that were redirected through legal manipulation. Someone is using philanthropy to launder stolen legacy.

The mystery reaches the group when Jasper recognizes a piece — a writing desk donated to a Fort Greene library that he last saw in his aunt's sitting room. An aunt who died six months ago under circumstances no one in his family questioned.

For the first time, Jasper can't deflect, joke, or chaos his way out. The investigation leads directly into his family's world — the old money networks, the family offices, the lawyers who make problems disappear. And the group finally learns who Jasper really is.

**Why Jasper:**
- The mystery is literally his family
- His secret (old money background) can no longer be hidden
- His chaos-as-rebellion origin story gets recontextualized — it wasn't just about being different, it was about knowing what that world actually does
- March as the setting gives him spring, rebirth, the possibility of being known and still accepted

**Character Arc:**
Jasper has spent years performing chaos to avoid becoming his family. This investigation forces him to use the very connections and knowledge he's been running from. He has to navigate formal dinners, family offices, and coded conversations — and the group sees him do it with terrifying fluency. The mask doesn't just slip; it comes off. His arc resolves the central tension of his character: you can reject the world you came from without pretending it doesn't exist. His friends knowing the truth doesn't trap him — it frees him.

**Secret Fully Revealed:** The old money background comes out completely. Not through confession (like Emma's in Book 1) but through evidence the group can't ignore — Jasper's ease in a world of inherited wealth, the family name that opens doors he pretends not to know about, the trust fund that's larger than anyone suspected. The group's reaction is complicated: Emma and Elijah feel the class gap acutely. Noah doesn't care. Olivia is hurt he didn't trust them. Oliver, characteristically, just nods — he'd already figured it out.

**Elijah as Host — Why It Works:**
The group has never been to Elijah's apartment. What they find surprises them: it's warm. Not in a decorating-magazine way, but in a lived-in way. Family photos on the fridge. His grandmother's quilt on the couch. Archery trophies on a shelf he'd never mention. The space reveals the Elijah he doesn't show at work or even always with the group. Hosting forces him to be vulnerable in a way that builds on his Book 3 growth.

**March Texture:** The first signs of spring. Crocuses in Prospect Park. The particular March light that's still cold but longer. St. Patrick's Day parade energy. The neighborhood waking up after winter — more foot traffic, outdoor seating tentatively returning, the farmers market gearing up for a new season. A year's worth of stories cycling back to where they started.

**Minor Character Opportunities:**
- Brenda Marquez returns (she moves in the same real estate/old money circles)
- New: A member of Jasper's family — a cousin or sibling who's still inside the world Jasper rejected
- New: An estate lawyer or archivist who's been quietly tracking the stolen items
- Hank makes a cameo (the farmers market is reopening for spring — he's back)

**Reputation Status:** By the end of Book 6, the Supper Club is a known entity along the G line. Not famous — but if you have a problem that's too weird for the cops and too small for a lawyer, someone will tell you about the dinner party group in Williamsburg. The final scene should acknowledge this without making it precious: maybe a stranger approaches Emma at the reopened farmers market and says, "You're one of those supper club people, right? I need to talk to you about something."
