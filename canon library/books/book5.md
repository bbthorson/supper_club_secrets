# Book 5: [Working Title TBD]

**Focal Character:** [Noah](../characters/noah.md)
**Month:** February
**Host:** Jasper (his Chelsea apartment — after Book 4 revealed it's actually a great space)

**Mystery Concept:**
A hyper-local neighborhood app — something like a Nextdoor competitor specifically for the G line corridor — has become wildly popular over the past few months. It helps people find babysitters, report broken streetlights, share restaurant recommendations. Everyone loves it. Noah championed it to the group.

Then strange things start happening. A local business gets review-bombed through the app after refusing to pay for a "premium listing." A tenant organizing meeting gets doxxed. A family's home address is leaked after they complained about a landlord. The app isn't just connecting the neighborhood — it's being weaponized.

Noah initially defends the platform (it's a technology problem with a technology solution). But as the investigation deepens, he discovers the app's data practices are predatory, its moderation is outsourced to people who don't live in the community, and the real business model is selling hyper-local behavioral data to real estate developers. The tech that was supposed to help the neighborhood is the tool being used to reshape it.

**Why Noah:**
- The mystery lives in his world — tech, platforms, digital infrastructure
- His core belief (technology solves problems) is directly challenged
- The investigation requires analog, human-intelligence methods he's always dismissed
- February + Valentine's Day creates comedy around his minimalist, anti-sentiment persona

**Character Arc:**
Noah has to confront the possibility that his worldview is not just incomplete but actively harmful. He championed this app. He told people to use it. The damage it's doing is, in part, his responsibility — a softer echo of Jasper's Book 1 mistake, which deepens their still-healing relationship. His arc isn't about abandoning tech, but about understanding that technology without community accountability is just another extraction tool.

**Secret Progression:** The sneaker collection gets exposed. The investigation leads to Noah's apartment (maybe the app's data reveals something about his building), and during a group scene, someone opens the wrong closet. The reveal is played for warmth, not humiliation — the group is surprised, then delighted. Elijah: "You own forty thousand dollars in shoes and you tried to serve us sulfur-free wine." Noah's hypocrisy becomes the thing that makes him most human.

**Valentine's Day Beat:** Noah is aggressively indifferent to Valentine's Day. The group finds this hilarious. Emma makes heart-shaped something as a joke. But the holiday gives the book an emotional undercurrent about what we value, what we collect, what we hold onto — connecting Noah's sneakers to the app's promise of "community" to the question of whether connection can be manufactured.

**February Texture:** Deep winter. The G line corridor at its most intimate — everyone bundled, moving fast, ducking into warm spaces. Jasper's Chelsea apartment becomes a refuge. Steamed-up windows. The particular silence of snow on Brooklyn streets (when it comes). Valentine's decorations in bodega windows.

**Minor Character Opportunities:**
- Sofia returns (her cheese shop was review-bombed on the app)
- New: The app's creator — a well-meaning developer who lost control of what they built
- New: A teenage coder from the neighborhood who figures out the data pipeline before Noah does

**Reputation Status:** The group is now sought out. The teenage coder finds them specifically. The app developer knows about them. Someone on the app has created a thread titled "who are those dinner party people who keep solving stuff?" Jasper screenshots it and frames it.
