# Book 6: [Working Title TBD]

**Focal Character:** [Jasper](../characters/jasper.md)
**Month:** March
**Host:** Elijah (his apartment — the group finally enters his world)

**Mystery Concept:**
A series of high-value items — antique furniture, rare books, a small but significant art collection — are being quietly donated to Brooklyn cultural institutions. Anonymous gifts, no strings attached, worth a small fortune collectively. It seems benevolent until the pattern reveals something darker: every item traces back to estates that were contested, families that were pressured, and inheritances that were redirected through legal manipulation. Someone is using philanthropy to launder stolen legacy.

The mystery reaches the group when Jasper recognizes a piece — a writing desk donated to a Fort Greene library that he last saw in his aunt's sitting room. An aunt who died six months ago under circumstances no one in his family questioned.

For the first time, Jasper can't deflect, joke, or chaos his way out. The investigation leads directly into his family's world — the old money networks, the family offices, the lawyers who make problems disappear. And the group finally learns who Jasper really is.

**Why Jasper:**
- The mystery is literally his family
- His secret (old money background) can no longer be hidden
- His chaos-as-rebellion origin story gets recontextualized — it wasn't just about being different, it was about knowing what that world actually does
- March as the setting gives him spring, rebirth, the possibility of being known and still accepted

**Character Arc:**
Jasper has spent years performing chaos to avoid becoming his family. This investigation forces him to use the very connections and knowledge he's been running from. He has to navigate formal dinners, family offices, and coded conversations — and the group sees him do it with terrifying fluency. The mask doesn't just slip; it comes off. His arc resolves the central tension of his character: you can reject the world you came from without pretending it doesn't exist. His friends knowing the truth doesn't trap him — it frees him.

**Secret Fully Revealed:** The old money background comes out completely. Not through a tidy confession but through evidence the group can't ignore — Jasper's ease in a world of inherited wealth, the family name that opens doors he pretends not to know about, the trust fund that's larger than anyone suspected. (Note: this differs from Book 1, where Emma's secret was *not* revealed — the opaque-secret direction means Jasper's reveal is the series' first full secret payoff.) The group's reaction is complicated: Emma and Elijah feel the class gap acutely. Noah doesn't care. Olivia is hurt he didn't trust them. Oliver, characteristically, just nods — he'd already figured it out.

**Elijah as Host — Why It Works:**
The group has never been to Elijah's apartment. What they find surprises them: it's warm. Not in a decorating-magazine way, but in a lived-in way. Family photos on the fridge. His grandmother's quilt on the couch. Archery trophies on a shelf he'd never mention. The space reveals the Elijah he doesn't show at work or even always with the group. Hosting forces him to be vulnerable in a way that builds on his Book 3 growth.

**March Texture:** The first signs of spring. Crocuses in Prospect Park. The particular March light that's still cold but longer. St. Patrick's Day parade energy. The neighborhood waking up after winter — more foot traffic, outdoor seating tentatively returning, the farmers market gearing up for a new season. A year's worth of stories cycling back to where they started.

**Minor Character Opportunities:**
- Brenda Marquez returns (she moves in the same real estate/old money circles)
- New: A member of Jasper's family — a cousin or sibling who's still inside the world Jasper rejected
- New: An estate lawyer or archivist who's been quietly tracking the stolen items
- Hank makes a cameo (the farmers market is reopening for spring — he's back)

**Reputation Status:** By the end of Book 6, the Supper Club is a known entity along the G line. Not famous — but if you have a problem that's too weird for the cops and too small for a lawyer, someone will tell you about the dinner party group in Williamsburg. The final scene should acknowledge this without making it precious: maybe a stranger approaches Emma at the reopened farmers market and says, "You're one of those supper club people, right? I need to talk to you about something."
