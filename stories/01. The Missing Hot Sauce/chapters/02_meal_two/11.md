### Chapter Eleven - Flash in the Pan

Oliver recited the number from his notes, his voice careful and precise: "2847-LLC. Filed October 18th."

Noah's eyes scanned the screen, his fingers moving in quick, deliberate bursts—opening tabs, running searches through databases that the rest of the group wouldn't have known existed. Corporate registries, patent filings, subsidiary mappings. The kind of digital archaeology that Noah did the way Oliver did physical research: methodically, relentlessly, with the quiet satisfaction of following a system to its logical conclusion.

"Okay... okay, wow." He looked up from the screen, and for the first time in the two years Emma had known him, Noah looked genuinely surprised. Not annoyed, not smugly confirmed—surprised. "This is not a small-time operation. That LLC is a subsidiary of a much larger hospitality group. One that was just granted a series of patents for..." He paused, and a smile crept across his face—not his usual smirk, but something warmer, almost childlike. "...get this... 'shelf-stable capsicum-based flavor enhancers.'"

"Hot sauce," Emma whispered, her eyes wide.

"Hot sauce," Noah confirmed, turning the laptop toward the group. The patent filing was dense with legalese, but the key phrases jumped out even to non-technical eyes: *proprietary capsaicin extraction*, *heritage pepper cultivar stabilization*, *shelf-stable artisanal flavor profile*. Someone had filed patents on the idea of Hank's hot sauce—not his specific recipe, but the category of product his recipe represented. It was like patenting the concept of a grandmother's cooking.

The room was electric. The scattered, flimsy threads of their individual investigations had woven themselves into a single, tangible tapestry. It wasn't aliens or the CIA or a socialite with a private jet. It was something worse because it was real—corporate predation dressed up in legal filings and numbered entities, the kind of quiet violence that didn't leave bruises but destroyed livelihoods all the same.

"They're trying to own the market for heritage hot sauce," Elijah said, the statement hanging in the air with a newfound weight. His voice had shifted from dry observation to something harder. "That's what this is. They're not just stealing his recipe. They're filing patents that would make it illegal for him to sell his own family's product."

"That's... that's insane," Olivia said, her theatrical energy gone, replaced by something rawer. "Can they do that?"

"Technically?" Elijah leaned forward, his elbows on his knees. "The patent system is designed to protect innovation, not tradition. A heritage recipe isn't automatically protected. If Hank never filed a trademark, never documented his process in a legal framework... then yes. Someone with better lawyers could claim the commercial rights to what his family has been making for four generations."

A wave of righteous anger washed over the group—the particular kind that comes from understanding injustice clearly enough to feel it personally. Emma looked at the patent filing on Noah's screen and thought about Dorothy's bottle, the handwritten label, the seeds from Virginia. She thought about Hank's face when he told the Fish Pepper story, the way his voice softened when he talked about his great-grandmother. And she thought about some faceless corporate entity filing paperwork to turn all of that into a line item on a balance sheet.

"I knew it," she said, and her voice was quiet but fierce. "It was Big Hot Sauce. I know I said it as a joke, but it was Big Hot Sauce."

"This is incredible," Noah said, looking up from his laptop with a genuine sense of wonder that he couldn't quite conceal behind his usual affect. "The data actually correlated. We followed the data and it led us to the truth." He couldn't hide the satisfaction in his voice; for once, the messy real world had aligned with the clean logic he preferred. The system had worked.

Olivia was already planning their next move, her voice buzzing with energy. "We have to tell someone! We have to expose them! This is a story! A real, live story!"

It was then that Jasper, who had been uncharacteristically quiet through all of this—no conspiracy theories, no wild gesticulations, no knocked-over wine glasses—stood up. He'd been waiting for this moment all evening, and now that it had arrived, he stood with his shoulders back and a triumphant smile that started at his mouth and reached his eyes in a way his usual grins never quite did.

"Way ahead of you," he announced.

The group turned to look at him.

"What do you mean?" Elijah asked, his brow furrowing.

"Well, when Oliver found that LLC, I couldn't just let it sit there," Jasper said, beaming. "So I called my friend Brenda in commercial real estate. Told her the whole story. And she just texted me back." He held up his phone like a trophy, the screen bright in the candlelit room. "She confirmed it. That LLC? It belongs to the restaurant mogul who's been buying up half of Williamsburg. She said he's notorious for this kind of thing—pressuring small vendors, acquiring recipes, using legal threats to clear the way for his own brands."

He looked around the room, expecting applause. This time, he got it.

"Jasper, you beautiful man!" Olivia cried out, raising her glass.

"I can't believe it actually worked," Noah said, shaking his head in disbelief—though whether he meant the investigation or Jasper specifically contributing something useful was unclear. Probably both.

Jasper took a theatrical bow, nearly knocking his wine glass off the counter. "Never doubt the network," he said with a wink.

The rest of the evening was a celebration. They opened Jasper's second bottle—the celebration bottle, which turned out to be significantly better than the research bottle, suggesting Jasper had been strategic for possibly the first time in his life. They toasted their success, their cleverness, their friendship. Emma put the apple crumble on the table and let people serve themselves, and for a while, the apartment was full of nothing but warmth and the clinking of glasses and the satisfied sounds of people eating dessert while feeling like they'd just done something that mattered.

"This is amazing," Emma said, raising her glass. The candlelight caught the wine, turning it ruby. "We actually did it. We're like real detectives."

"Amateur detectives," Oliver corrected with a small smile.

"The best kind," Jasper declared.

As the night wound down and they said their goodbyes—Olivia kissing Emma's cheek, Elijah offering a rare, firm handshake, Noah nodding with what passed for warmth in his repertoire—the group was buoyant, floating on a shared cloud of victory. They felt like they could do anything. They were a team. They were detectives. And they were completely, blissfully unaware that their cover had just been blown.

None of them noticed Jasper's phone buzz with a text from Brenda as he walked home that night, his hands shoved in his jacket pockets, his breath making small clouds in the cold October air:

*Hey, weird question—did you say Emma was leading this investigation? My contact asked. Just want to make sure I gave him the right info. Call me.*

Jasper, riding high on the evening's success and three glasses of wine, didn't see it until the next morning. He read it twice, frowned, and then got distracted by a notification about a 311 complaint involving a raccoon in a bodega.

By then, it was already too late.
