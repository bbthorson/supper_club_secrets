# Hank

## Overview
A charismatic hot sauce artisan and storyteller whose family recipe and theatrical personality make him a beloved fixture of the McGolrick Park farmers market — and the central figure of the group's first mystery.

## Personality
* Theatrical and warm — turns every customer interaction into a performance, complete with origin stories and dramatic flourishes
* Deeply proud of his family heritage, especially his great-grandmother's legacy
* Generous by nature — gives Dorothy a bottle of heritage sauce "just in case," shares his story freely with anyone who'll listen
* More vulnerable than he appears — when cornered by the mogul's legal threats, he retreats rather than fights
* Down-to-earth beneath the showmanship — when Jasper finds him at Meadowlight, he's quiet, humble, and grateful
* Stubborn about authenticity — refuses to sell out his family's name for a lowball offer, even when the alternative is walking away from everything

## Background

### Family History
Hank's great-grandmother migrated north from Virginia in the 1920s, settling in a row house in Philadelphia. She brought almost nothing with her — just her clothes and a handful of heirloom pepper seeds. Those seeds were Fish Peppers, a rare striped variety with deep roots in African-American culinary history along the Chesapeake Bay.

She grew them in a tiny plot behind her row house. The peppers, and the sauce she made from them, became a family tradition — passed down through four generations. Hank inherited both the seeds and the recipe, and with them, a sense of purpose.

### The Hot Sauce Business
Hank built his brand the old-fashioned way: one bottle at a time, sold face-to-face. He secured a stall at the McGolrick Park farmers market and became a fixture there for three years, never missing a single market day. His pitch was part carnival barker, part family historian — he'd tell customers about his great-grandmother, the Fish Pepper, the "secret family recipe," and make every bottle feel like a piece of living history.

His hot sauce also appeared on shelves at local boutiques and specialty shops in Williamsburg, including The Gilded Fern. The business wasn't making him rich, but it was honest work rooted in something real.

### The Offer and the Threat
A restaurant mogul operating through an anonymous LLC approached Hank with an offer — not for his recipe, but for the trademark to his family's name. The sum was insultingly low. When Hank tried to negotiate, the offer disappeared and was replaced by legal threats: stop selling, or be buried in litigation costs he couldn't afford.

Hank had no trademark protection (he'd never filed one — he was a farmer's market vendor, not a corporation). The mogul's hospitality group simultaneously filed patents for "shelf-stable capsicum-based flavor enhancers" — effectively attempting to own the commercial rights to what Hank had been making by hand for years.

### The Disappearance
Overwhelmed and afraid, Hank pulled his inventory from local shops, gave up his market stall, and fled to the Meadowlight Collective — a small intentional community about two hours west of Philadelphia where artists and growers live. They were the only people he knew who still grew the old striped Fish Pepper varieties. He retreated to the greenhouse, tending his great-grandmother's plants, trying to figure out what to do next.

### The Return
Jasper tracked him down through the Fish Pepper connection. Hank was moved to learn that a group of strangers had fought for his story. He agreed to come to the final dinner, where he met the group and shared his full story. A pro bono lawyer (who saw the viral coverage) helped him file a proper trademark. He filed his stall application for the following weekend.

## Skills
* Artisanal hot sauce production — small-batch, traditional methods using heirloom Fish Peppers
* Storytelling and salesmanship — can make a bottle of hot sauce feel like a family heirloom
* Heritage seed cultivation — understands heirloom pepper varieties, growing conditions, seed preservation
* Community building — three years of market relationships with vendors, shop owners, and regulars

## Relationships
* **Emma**: She's a regular customer and genuine fan. She doesn't just love his hot sauce — she loves the story behind it. Her desire to retell his narrative when serving his product is what kicks off the entire mystery.
* **Dorothy**: A fellow market vendor (honey) who he trusted enough to give a bottle of his heritage sauce before he disappeared. "His great-grandmother would've wanted her peppers shared."
* **Ruth**: Another market vendor (preserves) who noticed he was "off" in his final week. Represents the market community that cared about him.
* **Marcus (Gilded Fern)**: A boutique owner who carried his product. Hank paid him cash to break the shelf-space contract when he pulled his inventory.
* **The Mogul**: His antagonist. Hank never names him directly — the threat came through lawyers and corporate structures, not face-to-face confrontation.

## Character Arc
Hank begins the story as an absence — a ghost defined by the stories others tell about him. Through the investigation, he transforms from a missing person into a symbol of what the group is fighting for: the idea that a family's story and craft shouldn't be erased by corporate power. By the end, he's a real person again — grateful, grounded, and ready to reclaim his stall. His return validates the group's efforts and gives them a reason to keep going.

## Secrets
* The full extent of the legal threats he received is worse than what he tells the group. The mogul's lawyers didn't just threaten litigation — they implied they could make his life in Brooklyn untenable.
* He considered selling the recipe outright before fleeing. The guilt of almost giving up his great-grandmother's legacy is something he carries quietly.

## Notes
* Hank's age: mid-fifties ("older than Jasper expected"), with kind eyes and calloused hands.
* The Fish Pepper is a real heirloom variety — distinctive green and white striped skin, historically significant in African-American cooking.
* Hank may become a recurring character in future books — a grateful ally and market fixture who tips the group off to local mysteries.
* His great-grandmother's migration story (Virginia → Philadelphia, 1920s) is a specific historical detail that should remain consistent across all references.
