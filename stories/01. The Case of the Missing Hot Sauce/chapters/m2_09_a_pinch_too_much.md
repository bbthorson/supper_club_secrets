### Chapter Nine - A Pinch Too Much

Thursday evening, Noah finally decided to do his part. He was lounging on his minimalist grey sofa—the one piece of furniture in his apartment that had been chosen for aesthetic reasons rather than pure function, though he'd never admit that out loud. The apartment itself was a shrine to efficiency: clean lines, neutral colors, nothing on the walls except a single floating shelf that held exactly three books, all about systems design. His laptop was balanced on his knees, the screen reflecting against the window that looked out over a slice of the East River, the water catching the last copper light of the October evening.

The dinner party had been fun, a pleasant social algorithm with predictable inputs and outputs. The speculation about Hank was an amusing conversational loop—he'd even enjoyed riffing on the CIA pepper market theory, the way you enjoy a puzzle that doesn't have real stakes. But now, alone in his quiet, orderly apartment—where the only sounds were the hum of his air purifier and the distant, rhythmic churn of the building's HVAC—the problem felt fundamentally different. Theorizing over wine was one thing. Actual investigation required data, and data required a digital footprint.

He cracked his knuckles and got to work. He typed "Hank's Hot Sauce Brooklyn" into a search engine. The results were sparse: a few old blog posts with broken images, some tagged photos on social media from years ago, and a defunct business listing on Yelp with exactly three reviews—all five stars, all from the same month, all reading like they'd been written by someone's grandmother. He tried variations—"heritage pepper sauce Brooklyn," "fish pepper hot sauce vendor," "McGolrick Park market vendor"—cross-referencing against business registries, social media platforms, and every public database he could think of. Nothing. The man had no website, no LLC, no LinkedIn, no digital trail of any kind. It was like trying to grep a handwritten notebook.

"The guy's a ghost," he said to his empty apartment, a habit he'd developed from years of talking through problems with his code and never quite broken when the screen wasn't open. He stared at the screen, genuinely frustrated. This wasn't a problem he could solve with the tools he had. Hank existed in an entirely analog world—cash transactions, handshake deals, word-of-mouth reputation—and Noah's entire skill set was built for a digital one. It was like being handed a mystery written in a language he couldn't read.

He closed his laptop, not with satisfaction but with the specific irritation of a man who'd hit a wall he couldn't engineer his way around.

He opened his laptop again—not for Hank, but to check on a personal project, a code repository he maintained in his spare time. He told himself he was just reviewing a pull request, but his eyes drifted to the group chat. Elijah's text from earlier was still on his screen: *Financial model suggests... low-to-mid six figures. That's serious money.*

Noah frowned. Six figures wasn't "disappear without a trace" money, but it was "worth competing for" money. It was the kind of number that made rational actors behave rationally—and rational actors behaving rationally was Noah's entire worldview. If someone was going to do something shady for a reason that made economic sense, that was a problem he could understand. That was a system he could model.

He reopened his browser, this time with actual curiosity nagging at him. He started with the LLC number Oliver had shared in the group chat, running it through a few business registry databases he knew from work. The results were nested, layered—one LLC registered under another, which was registered under a holding company, which was registered in Delaware. Standard corporate obfuscation, the kind of thing that made him simultaneously annoyed and impressed by its architectural elegance.

Maybe there was more to this than he'd thought.

---

Friday afternoon, Jasper was vibrating with energy. He paced his chaotic apartment—a space that looked like a thrift store had gotten into a fight with a used bookstore, and both had lost. Mismatched furniture crowded every surface, and stacks of books on subjects ranging from urban beekeeping to Cold War espionage leaned at precarious angles against the walls. A half-finished jigsaw puzzle of the Williamsburg Bridge occupied the dining table. The only thing organized in the entire apartment was his vinyl collection, which was alphabetized and stored in milk crates with hand-written dividers.

For Jasper, the mystery wasn't a data problem or a financial model. It was a story—a narrative puzzle just waiting for its missing piece, the kind of thing that made his brain light up in ways that his day job at the 311 call center never did. He loved that job for what it lacked—expectations, ambition, the suffocating weight of family legacy—but it didn't feed this part of him. The part that needed to connect things, to see patterns in the chaos, to prove that his particular brand of scattered attention was actually a kind of intelligence the world hadn't figured out how to value yet.

When Oliver's group text came through with the LLC number, it felt like a starter pistol.

*OLIVER (Group): Found something. Anonymous LLC filed for waterfront restaurant two days after Hank disappeared. Filing #2847-LLC. Probably nothing but thought you should know.*

Jasper didn't hesitate. He grabbed his jacket and headed for the door, because Jasper thought better in motion—sitting still made his thoughts collide rather than connect. He ended up on a bench in McGolrick Park, the city's autumn energy buzzing around him: joggers, dog walkers, a couple arguing in Spanish about something that sounded serious but might have been about dinner. He scrolled through his phone, bypassing investigators and officials and anyone who might have been useful in a conventional sense, and landed on a name he hadn't contacted in years.

Brenda Chen. A friend from what Jasper privately called his "previous life"—the period before he'd opted out of his family's expectations and into a studio apartment and a 311 call center. Brenda had been part of that world, the world of networking events and business dinners and people who used "leverage" as a verb. She'd gone into commercial real estate—high-end stuff, the kind of deals where the properties had waterfront views and the clients had lawyers on retainer. They'd kept in touch the way people do when they genuinely like each other but inhabit different orbits: occasional texts, the rare phone call, a standing invitation to drinks that was extended more often than it was honored.

He tapped her name and put the phone to his ear. She picked up on the second ring.

"Brenda, my love!" he said with breathless energy. "I have the most bizarre question for you, it involves hot sauce, a missing person, and possibly a vast corporate conspiracy linked to the Illuminati's control of the global pepper market, you're not going to believe it..."

He spent the next ten minutes laying out the entire story with his signature chaotic flair—a whirlwind of speculation and half-facts that somehow managed to convey the core of their discoveries. He gave her the LLC number. He told her Emma had discovered the whole thing, named her as the driving force behind the investigation. He painted a picture of their ragtag group of amateur detectives with the kind of enthusiasm that made Brenda laugh despite herself.

Brenda, who had long ago learned to filter Jasper's narrative style for the essential details the way you'd pan for gold in a fast-moving river, was intrigued despite herself. "Jasper, you are a menace," she said, a smile in her voice. "But this is juicy. Let me make a call."

"You're the best!" Jasper beamed.

"Just... be careful, okay?" Brenda added, her tone shifting—the smile still there, but something underneath it, a weight that Jasper missed entirely because he was already thinking about Sunday's grand reveal. "The restaurant mogul I'm thinking of? He's ruthless. Really ruthless. If you're right about this, he won't take kindly to people poking around."

"Always am!" Jasper said cheerfully, already bouncing to his feet, already composing the narrative of his triumph. He could see it: the moment at dinner when he'd stand up, drop the name, and the room would erupt. It was going to be magnificent.

He hung up and headed home, his stride long and loose, the October wind catching his flannel jacket. He felt electric. Useful. Like the chaos in his head had, for once, produced something of genuine value—a connection nobody else could have made, a thread only he could have pulled.

He didn't notice the three missed calls from Brenda that would come later that evening, or the text she'd send at 11 PM that would sit unread on his lock screen all night.

Because Brenda, sitting at her desk at Apex Realty, had recognized the LLC number. She'd seen it in filings from a client her firm worked with—a very important, very litigious client whose business she couldn't afford to jeopardize. She'd weighed her options: her loyalty to an old friend versus her obligation to the firm that paid her mortgage. It wasn't even close, really. It was the kind of calculation that felt terrible and obvious at the same time.

She picked up her phone and made a call she would later regret.

"Hi, this is Brenda Chen from Apex Realty. I need to speak with someone about a potential issue with one of your Brooklyn acquisitions..."
