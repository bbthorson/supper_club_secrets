### Chapter Fourteen - The Seamless Supper

"Hey, you!" Olivia's voice was a bright, cheerful burst through the phone. "I was just thinking about you. Marcus at The Gilded Fern asked if we'd cracked the case yet. The whole neighborhood is talking—"

"Liv," Emma choked out, her voice a raw whisper. "Something's happened."

The cheer vanished like a light being switched off, replaced by a cold, sharp focus that Emma had only heard a handful of times — the Olivia underneath the performance, the one who'd once talked a friend through a car accident over the phone without raising her voice. "What is it? What's wrong?"

Emma tried to explain — the man at the door, the legal document, the handwritten note — but the words kept catching in her throat, coming out in ragged, panicked bursts that didn't quite form sentences. She could hear herself and knew she wasn't making sense.

Olivia listened without interrupting. When Emma finally managed to get the whole story out, stumbling over the legal terms and then reading the handwritten note in a voice that shook, Olivia's first words weren't of fear, but of fury.

"That son of a bitch," she hissed, her voice tight. "That cowardly little man hiding behind his lawyers." She took a deep breath — Emma could hear her consciously steadying herself. "Listen to me, Ems. This is what they do. I've had cease and desists from competitors for my candle line. It's a bully tactic. It means they're scared, not strong."

"But he knows, Liv," Emma whispered. "He knows about my... my account."

"He knows you're a smart, creative woman who found a way to make a living in a world that is not built for us," Olivia shot back. "That's all he knows. And we are not going to let him use it against you." A pause, the sound of her typing quickly in the background — Emma could picture her, phone wedged between shoulder and ear, already composing a group text with one hand while pulling on her shoes with the other. "Okay. I'm starting a group text. Everyone, your place, tonight. This is a five-alarm fire."

"But it's a Wednesday," Emma said weakly. "Elijah's probably working, Noah has his coding thing—"

"I don't care if Noah is debugging the space shuttle," Olivia said, her voice leaving no room for argument. "They'll get here when they get here. I'm on my way."

The line went dead. Emma stood in the silence of her kitchen, the phone still pressed to her ear, listening to nothing. The lemon zest was still scattered across the floor. The biscuit dough was hardening on the counter. Outside, the October afternoon was doing what October afternoons always did — the light softening, the shadows lengthening, the world carrying on as if nothing had changed. For the first time since the knock, she felt a tiny, fragile flicker of hope. She wasn't alone. The cavalry was coming.

---

An hour later, the mood in Emma's apartment was funereal. The candles from last Sunday had burned down to waxy stumps, and no one had thought to light new ones. The overhead fluorescent was on instead, casting that pale, institutional light that made everything look slightly worse than it was.

Olivia had arrived first, enveloping Emma in a fierce hug that lasted long enough to say everything words couldn't, before immediately taking charge. She'd taken the legal document from Emma's hands, read it twice, set it on the coffee table, and said, "We're going to need everyone."

Oliver and Noah had arrived together soon after, their faces etched with a shared, anxious concern that looked different on each of them — Oliver pale and withdrawn, Noah rigid with the discomfort of a man who preferred problems with clean solutions. The legal document sat on the coffee table, a toxic centerpiece. They had all read it. They had all seen the handwritten note. Now they were sitting in a heavy, suffocating silence, the reality sinking in like cold water.

"We should eat," Olivia said finally, breaking the silence. "We need to eat."

Emma looked around at the legal document, the worried faces, the chaos of her apartment — flour still on the counter, the biscuit ingredients still out, the whole scene looking like a crime scene of interrupted domesticity. "Yeah," she said, her voice flat. "Let me just redd up a little first." The Pittsburghese slipped out before she could catch it, and she didn't bother correcting herself. She was past caring.

The suggestion to order food — simple, practical, the kind of thing that should have taken sixty seconds — immediately devolved into stress-fueled chaos.

"What are we in the mood for?" Oliver asked, already scrolling through a delivery app on his phone, relieved to have a task that didn't involve legal documents.

"I don't care," Emma mumbled from the couch, where she'd wrapped herself in a blanket that Olivia had draped over her shoulders.

"Okay, but we need to pick something," Noah said. "There are six of us, seven dietary opinions, and if we start debating this turns into a forty-minute ordeal. Someone just decide."

"Fine," Olivia said. "Thai."

"But—" Noah started, then caught the look on Olivia's face and recalibrated. "Thai works."

"Wait," Jasper said. He'd arrived moments before, breathless, still wearing his 311 lanyard, and had taken in the scene with an expression that cycled through confusion, horror, and something darker that he hadn't yet named. "We are never, ever, doing a group pizza order again. Not after the 2nd Avenue Debacle."

"Nobody said pizza, Jasp," Olivia said.

"I'm just establishing the ground rule."

A flicker of a shared, traumatic memory passed between him and Noah. Olivia shuddered. "He's right. Forty-five minutes. Six pizzas. Three untouched. I still have nightmares about the Hawaiian."

"Thai it is," Olivia announced with finality, scrolling through the app. "Pad thai, spring rolls, green curry, basil fried rice. I'm not discussing this further." Her thumb pressed confirm.

The door buzzed. Elijah. He stepped into the apartment, immediately loosening his tie — the good one, the one he wore for important meetings, which meant he'd come straight from the office. His eyes swept the room: the legal document on the table, Emma wrapped in a blanket, the others arrayed in various postures of worry and helplessness. He didn't need to ask. He picked up the document and began reading.

He stood by the window after finishing, his back to the room, silhouetted against the darkening October sky. When he turned around, his face was a mask of cold, analytical fury — the kind that looked like calm if you didn't know him, but that his friends recognized as the most dangerous version of Elijah. The one that did math while his jaw was clenched.

"Walk me through it," he said, not to Emma but to Olivia. "Chronologically. What happened, when, in what order."

Olivia recounted it: the knock, the process server, the document, the handwritten note. As she spoke, Elijah pulled out his phone and started typing — notes, probably in that spreadsheet app he used for everything from grocery lists to financial models.

"And you didn't sign anything," he confirmed. "You didn't agree to anything."

"No. I just... closed the door."

"Good." He pocketed his phone. "That's good."

Noah, who had been uncharacteristically quiet since the food was settled, was hunched over his laptop in the corner of the couch. He hadn't said a word since sitting down, which was unusual — normally he'd have three opinions by now. Instead he was just typing, his face lit by the screen, his expression the focused blankness of someone pulling at a thread.

"Noah?" Olivia said. "You okay over there?"

"Looking up cease and desist law," he said without looking up. "Precedents. How these things actually play out versus how they're designed to feel." He paused to scroll. "Most of these never go anywhere. They're built to scare you into compliance before anyone has to step into a courtroom."

"That's... not actually comforting right now," Emma said from inside her blanket.

"It should be." Noah looked up. "Because it means they don't have a real case. They have a letter and a bad attitude."

Oliver, sitting in the armchair near the window, said nothing. He was watching the room with that quiet, observant intensity that Emma sometimes forgot he had — the same focus he'd brought to the Department of Buildings database, now turned on the people he loved. His hands were folded in his lap, very still. He hadn't touched his phone once.

Jasper, meanwhile, was vibrating with barely contained energy — but it was a different kind than usual. Wrong-colored, somehow. He kept starting sentences and stopping, looking at the document on the table, then away. He'd made two cups of tea and drunk neither. The mugs sat on the counter, their steam long gone.

"You okay, Jasp?" Emma asked.

"Fine," he said too quickly. "Just — processing. This is a lot."

Elijah looked up from his notes. "This is toothless," he said finally, his voice dangerously quiet. He tossed the document back on the table. "It's a scare tactic. Well-drafted, expensive, designed to make you think you need a lawyer who charges eight hundred an hour. Which is probably what they paid theirs."

"Tell that to the part about my OnlyFans," Emma said, her voice muffled by the blanket.

Jasper, who had missed the initial explanation, looked confused. "Your what?"

Emma sat up slowly, pulling the blanket tight around her shoulders like armor. She took a shaky breath. This was the moment she'd been dreading — not the legal threat, which was terrifying but abstract, but this. These specific people, in this specific room. The moment where her friends would see her differently.

"It's not—" she started, her voice rising defensively, the words tumbling out before she could organize them. "Look, it's weird, okay? I know it's weird. But I'm not doing anything illegal. I'm just cooking. In my own kitchen. Barefoot. And apparently that's a thing for some people, and those people pay me, and that money is the only reason I'm not back in Pennsylvania living in my parents' basement right now."

She braced herself. For the judgment. The awkward silence. The careful diplomatic distance that would tell her everything she needed to know.

"So what?" Olivia said immediately, without hesitation, her voice fierce and absolute. "It's your body, it's your kitchen. If some weirdo wants to pay you to watch you make pasta, that's their problem, not yours."

"Doesn't matter if you were naked," Jasper chimed in, and the earnestness in his voice was so unlike his usual performance that it caught Emma off guard. "It's still your business. It's still your choice."

Elijah, who had been silent, finally spoke. There was no judgment in his eyes. Only a quiet, surprising flicker of respect. "You found a gap in the market and filled it with almost no startup cost," he said, his voice measured. "That's just good business." He paused, a dry smile crossing his face. "Though I have to say, 'Kinky Kitchen' is a significantly better brand name than 'E. Miller Financial Consulting.'"

"Are you saying you're jealous of Emma's foot fetish empire?" Jasper asked, a ghost of his usual grin returning.

"I'm saying she found a more creative revenue stream than I did," Elijah corrected. "There's a difference."

Emma felt something loosen in her chest — a knot she hadn't realized she'd been carrying for months, maybe longer. They weren't looking at her with pity or disgust. They were just... her friends. Still. The same people who'd argued about pizza toppings and wine choices and conspiracy theories. The same people who'd shown up on a Wednesday night because she needed them.

"But how did they even find it?" Olivia asked, her practical mind already working the problem. "Your account is anonymous, right?"

Emma nodded. "Different email, different name, I never show my face. But my Instagram cooking videos are public. They're under my real name. If someone was really digging, really looking into my finances, my online presence..." She trailed off, the implications settling over the room like a cold draft. "They must have hired someone. A private investigator, maybe. To find leverage."

"That's fucked up," Olivia said quietly, and the simplicity of it — no theatrical emphasis, no strategic pause — told Emma how angry she really was.

Elijah's phone buzzed. He glanced at the screen, and something flickered across his face — frustration, guilt, something complicated and private.

"I need to take this," he said, already standing. "Two minutes."

He stepped into Emma's tiny hallway, his back to the living room, voice low. The others couldn't hear the words, but they could hear the tone: patient, measured, the voice of someone who'd had this conversation before. The voice of someone who was always the responsible one.

When he came back, his jaw was tight.

"Everything okay?" Olivia asked.

"My mom," Elijah said shortly. "My sister's car broke down again. She needs money for the repair." He sat back down, rubbing his temples. "I told her I'd send it tomorrow."

"Elijah..." Emma started.

"It's fine," he said, and his voice made it clear the subject was closed — a door shut firmly but not slammed. "Family stuff. It doesn't matter right now. What matters is figuring out what we do about this." He gestured at the legal document. "We can worry about my family drama later."

But Emma caught the way his shoulders stayed tense, the way his eyes returned to his phone for just a moment before he pocketed it. She knew that look. It was the look of someone carrying more weight than they let on — someone who'd been carrying it for so long that the effort had become invisible, even to himself.
