## Meal Four

### Chapter Sixteen - Turning Up the Heat

The next forty-eight hours were a blur of coordinated, caffeine-fueled chaos. Emma's apartment, which had been the site of so much anxiety, transformed into a makeshift war room. Every horizontal surface was occupied—laptops on the kitchen counter, phones charging on the windowsill, notepads and printed documents covering the coffee table. Olivia's candles had been replaced by takeout coffee cups, and the scent of dread had been replaced by the electric smell of overheating laptops and the sharp, bitter aroma of coffee that was too strong because nobody had time to measure the grounds properly.

Noah was in his element, a digital architect constructing their invisible fortress. He'd claimed the armchair as his command center, three browser windows open, a terminal running in the background, his fingers moving with the focused intensity of someone solving a problem that actually mattered. "The key is to create layered redundancies," he explained to the group, most of whom were nodding along with the polite incomprehension of people watching a magician explain a card trick. "Each anonymous account routes through a different VPN node, uses a different email provider, and has its own unique metadata profile. We're not just sending emails; we're creating a distributed, ephemeral presence that can't be traced back to a single source."

"I understand maybe thirty percent of what you just said," Elijah remarked from his spot on the couch, where he was assembling the evidence package with the meticulous care of a man building a legal brief, "but I'm choosing to believe it's impressive."

"Think of it like cooking," Noah said, and both Emma and Oliver looked up, surprised. "You don't just throw all the ingredients into one pot. You prep them separately, season them individually, and combine them at the exact right moment for maximum impact."

Emma stared at him. "Did you just use a food metaphor?"

"Don't get used to it," Noah said, already back to typing.

Olivia and Emma sat side by side at the kitchen counter, a formidable duo of analog and digital networking. Olivia scrolled through her contacts—an address book that would have made a professional publicist weep with envy—identifying the neighborhood gossips, local tastemakers, and small-press food writers who would be most receptive to the story. She'd organized them into tiers on a spreadsheet that Elijah had helped her build, color-coded by reach, reliability, and likelihood of publishing.

Emma, meanwhile, was focused on the national food scene, her years in restaurant kitchens providing a map of the industry's internal politics that no amount of social media research could replicate. "Okay, this blogger hates that one," she'd say, pointing to a name on Olivia's list. "If we send it to both of them, one will publish it just to spite the other."

"Weaponized pettiness," Elijah observed from the couch. "Now that's a business model I can respect."

"It's how the food world works," Emma said. "Grudges are the real currency. People who got passed over for reviews, chefs who lost their sous chefs to bigger restaurants, food writers who got scooped on a story. If you know who owes who and who hates who, you can make information move faster than any press release."

Oliver and Elijah were the quiet, steady core of the operation. Oliver had printed out every document from his LLC research and organized them chronologically on the coffee table, cross-referenced with public filings and permit records. Elijah had stripped the financial model down to its most devastating essentials—a clean, one-page summary showing the mogul's pattern of acquiring and shuttering small brands, with projected costs and profit margins that made the exploitation impossible to dismiss.

Together, they assembled the press kit: a simple, powerful package that told Hank's story through data rather than emotion. The anonymous LLC filing. The patent applications for "shelf-stable capsicum-based flavor enhancers." The timeline showing the correlation between Hank's disappearance and the waterfront restaurant permit. Elijah's financial analysis. And a brief, unsigned narrative that connected the dots without making any claims that couldn't be supported by the attached evidence.

"This feels very 'corporate presentation,'" Jasper said, looking over their shoulders. He'd been hovering all morning, making coffee nobody asked for and straightening papers that didn't need straightening—the restless energy of a man who desperately wanted to be useful and was afraid of making things worse by trying.

"That's because corporations respond to corporate language," Elijah said without looking up. "We're speaking their dialect. The dialect of 'oh shit, this could cost us money.'"

By Friday morning, they were ready. The apartment was quiet for the first time in two days. Five laptops open, five pairs of eyes on screens, the press kit loaded into Noah's anonymous distribution system. Emma's finger hovered over the trackpad.

"Any second thoughts?" she asked.

"Several," Elijah said. "All of them irrelevant. Hit send."

Emma pressed the button. For a moment, nothing happened—just the soft whir of data traveling from her apartment to servers scattered across the world, bouncing through VPN nodes and anonymous relays before landing in the inboxes of a hundred strangers. The swarm was released.

---

The initial results were a trickle, not a flood. A few small Brooklyn blogs picked up the story, framing it as a local curiosity—"Missing Hot Sauce Vendor Tied to Shady LLC?" with varying levels of accuracy and enthusiasm. The group refreshed their browsers obsessively, tracking every mention, every share, every comment thread that sprouted beneath the posts.

"This feels anticlimactic," Jasper said, sprawled on the floor, his phone held above his face.

"That's because real change is boring," Elijah said, not looking up from his own screen. "It's not a movie montage. It's just... waiting."

"The dissemination curve follows a logarithmic function," Noah added. "The first few hours are always slow. Momentum builds exponentially once the story reaches a critical mass of sharing nodes."

"So we wait," Emma said.

"We wait."

They waited. Emma stress-cleaned her kitchen. Olivia organized Emma's spice rack by cuisine, then by color, then alphabetically. Noah ran diagnostics on his VPN setup for the fourth time. Oliver quietly read a Wikipedia article about the history of trademark law in the United States, taking notes. Elijah answered work emails with the strained efficiency of a man trying to maintain two parallel lives.

Then came the pushback.

Olivia saw it first—a notification from a food industry account she followed. "Oh no," she said, her voice flat.

A conservative business commentator with a surprisingly large online following had posted a scathing critique. His avatar was a man in a blazer, his bio read "Free Markets. Free Minds. Free Range," and his post was gaining traction fast.

*"Heard a rumor about a 'beloved' hot sauce vendor being 'pushed out' by a successful restaurant group. Translation: an entrepreneur built a better product and out-competed someone. This is called capitalism, not a crime. Maybe if the vendor had spent less time being 'theatrical' and more time trademarking his IP, he wouldn't be in this position. #FreemarketFriday"*

Elijah read it aloud, his voice carefully neutral. When he finished, he looked up at the group. "Apparently we're anti-capitalist now. Which is ironic, considering I literally work for capitalism." He took a sip of his coffee, which had gone cold two hours ago. "This is going to get messy."

A wave of doubt washed over the room—the specific, suffocating kind that comes from seeing your story reframed by someone who doesn't know the facts but has a bigger audience than you'll ever have. The commentator's post was dismissive and cynical, but it was also a plausible, powerful narrative. For a moment, their righteous anger was replaced by a familiar fear. What if people didn't see a hero? What if they just saw a failed businessman who should have hired a lawyer?

"We have to respond," Noah said immediately, his fingers already hovering over his keyboard. "I can draft a point-by-point rebuttal, dismantle his logical fallacies, show the data—"

"No," a quiet voice said. Oliver. He hadn't looked up from his screen, but his voice cut through the rising panic with the same calm authority he'd shown in the crisis meeting. The Oliver who emerged in pressure, the one his friends were still getting used to.

Everyone turned to look at him.

"Engaging directly is counterproductive," he said, his tone measured. "It elevates his platform and frames the conversation as a debate between two equal sides. The optimal strategy is not to rebut his thesis, but to provide our allies with the tools to dismantle it themselves."

He turned his laptop around. On the screen was a document he'd been quietly building for the past twelve hours—a meticulously organized set of talking points, complete with bullet points, cited sources, and pre-written responses to the most likely counter-arguments. It was the kind of document that looked simple on the surface but represented hours of careful, systematic thinking—an Oliver document, through and through.

"I've prepared a rebuttal framework," he said, and the phrase "I've taken the liberty" was so perfectly Oliver that Emma almost smiled despite the tension. "Point one: the prohibitive cost of trademark litigation for independent artisans. The filing fee alone is $350 per class, and that's before you account for legal representation, which averages $275 per hour for intellectual property matters. For a vendor whose gross revenue is under fifty thousand a year, that's a barrier designed to be insurmountable."

He was on his feet now, pacing—something none of them had ever seen Oliver do. "Point two: there are documented cases of trademark trolling by large restaurant groups in three of the five boroughs in the last decade alone. This isn't an isolated incident; it's a business model. Point three: the specific timeline of this particular case—the LLC filing, the patent applications, the waterfront permit—is inconsistent with legitimate competition and consistent with predatory acquisition."

He looked around the room. "We don't engage with the commentator. We send this document to every outlet that's already published the story, and to every outlet that hasn't. We give them the ammunition to respond in their own voice. We don't fight one battle. We start a hundred smaller ones, all with the same fact-based script."

Elijah looked at the document, then at Oliver. "Did you just solve a PR crisis with a Wikipedia editing strategy?"

"It's how consensus is built online," Oliver said, his cheeks flushing slightly. "You don't win arguments by yelling louder. You win them by providing better sources."

"That is a brilliant system," Noah said, and the respect in his voice was genuine.

They sent Oliver's talking points out into the digital ecosystem, a second wave layered on top of the first. For a few hours, nothing happened. The silence was agonizing. Emma paced. Noah refreshed his analytics dashboard every thirty seconds, the numbers barely moving. Jasper stress-ate an entire bag of chips and half a sleeve of crackers. Elijah had moved on to checking his work email, his face a mask of practiced calm that wasn't fooling anyone.

"How are you not freaking out?" Emma asked him.

"I am," Elijah said without looking up from his phone. "I'm just expensive enough at compartmentalizing that you can't tell."

The afternoon dragged. The light through Emma's windows shifted from noon-bright to the amber of late afternoon to the blue-gray of early evening. They ordered more coffee. Then food—pizza, because nobody had the energy to argue about it, and even the ghost of the 2nd Avenue Pizza Debacle couldn't compete with their collective exhaustion.

It was Olivia who saw it first. She was scrolling through her social media feed, her face slack with the particular expression of someone who'd been refreshing the same three apps for six hours straight. Then her thumb stopped moving. Her eyes went wide.

"Oh my god," she breathed.

"What is it?" Emma asked, crossing the room in three steps.

Olivia held up her phone. On the screen was a post from one of the country's most respected food critics—a woman known for her devastating reviews, her unwavering support for small artisans, and a social media following that made restaurant groups nervous. She hadn't just shared their story. She had adopted their argument as her own, rewritten it in her voice, and aimed it at her audience of three hundred thousand followers with the precision of a scalpel.

"Let's be clear," Olivia read aloud, her voice trembling with excitement. "'What's happening to this Brooklyn hot sauce vendor is not capitalism. It is a textbook case of predatory trademark trolling designed to stifle competition from the artisans who make our food culture worth celebrating. The prohibitive cost of litigation is a weapon, and it's being wielded by people who've forgotten they were once small, too.'"

A stunned silence.

"She used our words," Oliver said, and there was a quiet note of wonder in his voice—the sound of a man who'd spent years writing careful, well-sourced arguments in corners of the internet where nobody knew his name, watching someone with actual influence amplify them to the world. "Our exact phrasing."

Before they could process the magnitude of that single post, Noah, who had been typing furiously, spun his laptop around. On his screen was a real-time analytics graph showing social media sentiment around their story. For the past several hours, it had been a messy, inconclusive swirl of green, red, and gray. But in the last fifteen minutes, a massive spike of green had erupted like a wave breaking—one post, reshared, requoted, screenshot, and amplified across platforms, each share carrying Oliver's framework like a message in a hundred different bottles.

"It's working," Noah said, his voice tight with barely contained excitement. "Sentiment flipped twenty minutes ago and it's still climbing. The critic's post has been shared twelve hundred times. The commentator's take is getting ratioed into oblivion. And—" He scrolled down, his eyes widening. "Two of the mogul's major investors just issued statements distancing themselves from the project."

"Economic consequences," Elijah said with satisfaction. "The only language these people actually speak."

What happened next was fast, chaotic, and deeply satisfying. More outlets picked up the story. The Brooklyn food bloggers were first, then the Manhattan food press, then a national food publication that ran a long-form piece titled "The Hot Sauce Underground: How a Brooklyn Vendor's Disappearance Exposed a Pattern of Corporate Predation." The mogul's PR team issued a carefully worded non-response that only made things worse. A local news crew showed up at the vacant waterfront property and filmed a segment. Emma's phone buzzed with text after text from food industry contacts she hadn't heard from in months, all saying variations of the same thing: *Holy shit. Is this real?*

It was real.

Jasper raised his glass—they'd moved on to celebratory wine at some point, because some traditions were sacred even in the middle of a revolution. "To Oliver! The quiet genius!"

"To the swarm!" Olivia added.

"To extremely petty food bloggers!" Emma chimed in.

They clinked glasses, the apartment filling with laughter and relief and the intoxicating, dizzying feeling of having actually won something that mattered. Of having done something that made a difference, not because they were powerful or connected or rich, but because they were stubborn and clever and cared enough to try.

Elijah raised his glass last. "To the fact that we're all probably unemployable after this," he said with a dry smile. "But at least we have each other."

"And hot sauce," Jasper added. "When we find it."

"Details," Noah said, and for the first time, the word sounded like a joke instead of an objection.

The swarm had found its sting. And for the first time in weeks, Emma stood in her kitchen and felt something she'd almost forgotten existed: possibility. Not the anxious, scrambling kind that had defined her life since leaving Bistro Lavande. But the good kind—the kind that comes from knowing you're not alone, and that the people standing next to you aren't going anywhere.
