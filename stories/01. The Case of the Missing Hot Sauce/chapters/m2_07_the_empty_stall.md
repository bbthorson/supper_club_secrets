### Chapter Seven - The Empty Stall

Saturday morning, Emma found herself back at the McGolrick Park Greenmarket. She wasn't sure why. She had no shopping list, no recipe to test, no article deadline that required seasonal ingredients. She'd told herself she was going for the exercise, for the fresh air, for the routine of it—but those were lies she was telling with diminishing conviction. She was going because she needed to see.

The walk from her apartment took seven minutes. She'd done it enough times that her feet knew the route by muscle memory—right on North 12th, left on Bedford, straight through to the park entrance where the white tents started. The October air had a bite to it this morning, sharper than last week, with that metallic edge that said winter was closer than the calendar admitted. She'd thrown on a jacket she should have washed and the knit hat Olivia had given her last Christmas, the one with the pom-pom she kept meaning to cut off but secretly liked.

The market was in full swing, the usual Saturday crowd negotiating the narrow aisles between stalls. Young families with strollers that could have doubled as small SUVs. Hungover twentysomethings in sunglasses clutching coffees like talismans against the morning. Serious home cooks with their canvas bags and mental checklists, the kind of people who knew the difference between Delicata and Kabocha without reading the signs. Emma walked the familiar perimeter, past the apple stand where an old man was stacking Fujis with an architect's precision, past the bread vendor whose sourdough she could smell from twenty feet away, past the woman who sold those overpriced lavender sachets that Olivia loved and Emma pretended not to.

And then she was standing in front of the empty space.

Hank's spot. The prime corner location that caught morning light and foot traffic from both entrances. It was just... gone. Not repurposed, not filled by another vendor. Just an empty square of concrete between the pickle guy and the honey lady, like a missing tooth in an otherwise healthy smile. Someone had dragged the plastic barriers closer together to narrow the gap, but it still looked wrong—a hole in the market's fabric that the eye kept snagging on.

"Looking for Hank?"

Emma turned. The woman at the neighboring stall—Dorothy, according to her hand-painted sign—was watching her with knowing eyes. She was in her sixties, with silver-streaked hair pulled back in a practical braid and hands that told the story of decades of physical work—weathered, strong, the knuckles slightly swollen. She was surrounded by rows of golden honey jars, each one catching the thin morning sunlight like small amber lanterns. The air around her stall smelled sweet and waxy, undercut by something wilder—the faint, green scent of whatever herbs she'd planted near her hives.

"Yeah," Emma said, stepping closer. "I was hoping maybe... I don't know. That he'd be back."

Dorothy shook her head slowly, her expression settling into the careful neutrality of someone who'd seen enough of the world to know that hope and evidence were different currencies. "Haven't seen him in two weeks. Not like him. Man never missed a market day in three years, rain or shine." She paused, arranging a row of honeycomb sections in their wax paper wrappers with the absent-minded precision of long practice. "You're one of his regulars?"

"I am. Was." Emma hesitated, not sure how much to say. She wasn't an investigator. She was a chef who'd lost her favorite hot sauce vendor and had somehow turned it into a group project. But Dorothy's expression was open, patient, and—Emma noticed—a little worried. "Did he seem okay to you? The last time you saw him?"

Dorothy's hands paused on the honeycomb. Something shifted behind her eyes—not alarm, but a kind of recognition, as if Emma had asked the question she'd been waiting for someone to ask.

"He seemed... scared." The word sat between them, heavier than it should have been on a bright Saturday morning at a farmers market. "Kept looking over his shoulder like he was waiting for someone. Not the way you look when you're expecting a friend. The way you look when you're expecting trouble." Dorothy paused. "There was a man who came around a few weeks before that. Nice suit, good shoes, real charming. Bought a jar of my honey and a bottle of Hank's sauce and told Hank it was the best thing he'd ever tasted. Sent a handwritten note to my stall the next week — a *thank you note*, for honey." She shook her head. "You don't send a handwritten note about honey unless you want something." She reached under her table and pulled out a small, unlabeled bottle of deep red hot sauce. The glass was warm from being near the space heater under her table, and the sauce inside had that distinctive clarity—not the murky, mass-produced consistency of a supermarket brand, but something alive, with visible flecks of pepper suspended in a crimson medium.

"He gave me this," Dorothy said, turning it over in her hands. "Said his great-grandmother would've wanted her peppers shared. Told me to keep it. 'Just in case.'"

"In case of what?"

"He didn't say." Dorothy's voice was quiet, almost careful. "But that's not the kind of thing you say when you're going on vacation, is it?"

Emma stared at the bottle, at the handwritten label with its careful cursive: *Heritage Pepper Sauce - Small Batch - Hank*. Such a simple thing. A glass bottle, a homemade label, a few ounces of sauce. But holding it—Dorothy had pressed it into her hands without being asked, a transfer that felt oddly ceremonial—Emma could feel the weight of what it represented. This wasn't a product. It was a lineage. Seeds carried north from Virginia in the 1920s, planted in a tiny Philadelphia plot, tended through four generations, brought to Brooklyn by a man who loved the story as much as the sauce. And now it was sitting in her palm like an artifact from a history that was in danger of disappearing.

"Did he ever talk about his great-grandmother?" she asked quietly.

Dorothy smiled, the first warmth Emma had seen from her. The lines around her eyes deepened in a way that made her look both older and kinder. "Oh, all the time. He'd tell anyone who'd listen—customers, other vendors, the guy who came to fix the tent poles. She came up from Virginia in the twenties, brought nothing but her clothes and those pepper seeds. Grew them in a tiny plot in Philly, passed them down through the family. Hank used to say she'd be proud to see her peppers feeding Brooklyn." The smile faded. "He really loved that story. Loved telling it. That's how I knew something was wrong. Last time he was here, he didn't tell it once."

Emma felt something tighten in her chest. She'd always admired Hank's theatrical sales pitch, but she'd assumed it was marketing—a clever hook to sell sauce, the kind of origin story that food writers like her loved to feature because it made for good copy. Standing here now, in the empty space where he used to perform that pitch every weekend, she realized it was more than that. It was heritage. It was identity. It was the kind of story you only stop telling when something has broken inside you.

"If you see him," Dorothy said, her voice firm, "you tell him we miss him. The market's not the same without his hollering."

"I will," Emma said. "I promise."

She walked home slowly, the bottle of hot sauce in her jacket pocket, its weight pressing against her hip with each step. The market noise faded behind her—the calls of vendors, the rustle of bags, a child's delighted shriek at something—replaced by the quieter sounds of the neighborhood waking up. A dog barking from a fire escape. The distant rumble of the L train. Her own footsteps on the sidewalk, steady and deliberate, as if slowing down might help her think.

Whatever had happened to Hank, it wasn't just a business decision. Someone had scared him badly enough to give away his sauce, to abandon the market he loved, to disappear without a word. And the fact that he'd said "just in case"—those two small words that people only use when they're preparing for a version of the future they hope won't happen—told her that he'd seen it coming. Whatever "it" was.

The mystery had just gotten a lot more personal.
