### Chapter Seventeen - Farm to Table

While the digital war was being waged from Emma's living room, Jasper stood in his chaotic apartment on Saturday morning, feeling utterly useless. His phone buzzed with updates from the group chat—Noah's sentiment graphs, Olivia's influencer confirmations, the food critic's devastating endorsement. Victory after victory, all happening without him. The rest of them had found their roles, and every text was a reminder that his role, this time, was somewhere else.

He'd caused this mess. The least he could do was fix the part that mattered most.

Jasper closed his eyes, trying to remember that first dinner party—Emma's kitchen, the sizzle of onions, the smell of roasting squash, and her voice, tipsy and animated, describing Hank's theatrical pitch. What had she said? Something about a pepper. A weird pepper with a weird name.

*"...and he swears the secret is this crazy pepper his great-grandmother brought up from Philly, calls it a 'Fish Pepper' or something, says it's the only thing that gives it that... zing..."*

Fish Pepper. That was it.

He grabbed his laptop and started searching, the familiar feeling of a new rabbit hole pulling him forward. Within ten minutes, he'd confirmed it was real—a rare heirloom variety with distinctive green-and-white striped skin, historically significant in African-American culinary history along the Chesapeake Bay. The seeds had been passed down through families for generations, nearly going extinct in the mid-twentieth century before a small network of seed savers revived the variety. You couldn't just buy these at a garden center. You had to know someone. You had to be part of the story.

And knowing someone—being part of stories—was Jasper's actual superpower.

He started calling. Fish Peppers were a Chesapeake Bay variety—Maryland, Virginia, up into Pennsylvania—so he cast a wide net. Small nurseries in Maryland first, then Pennsylvania. His approach was pure Jasper—equal parts charm and barely controlled chaos, a human search engine that ran on enthusiasm and the unshakeable belief that if you called enough people, eventually one of them would know something useful.

"Hi, yes, I'm looking for a... well, it's complicated, but there's this guy, a hot sauce artisan, and he's gone missing, and I think he might be growing Fish Peppers, which I know sounds insane, but—"

Click. Dead line.

He tried again. And again. Most people hung up within thirty seconds. A few listened politely before explaining they'd never heard of such a person. One elderly man in Baltimore spent twenty minutes telling Jasper about his own pepper garden before admitting he'd never grown Fish Peppers in his life, but would Jasper like to hear about his Carolina Reapers? A woman at a heritage seed collective in Delaware put him on hold for fifteen minutes, then came back to say she couldn't help but wished him luck with "the pepper man situation."

By Tuesday afternoon, Jasper was on his twentieth call, sitting on a bench in McGolrick Park—not far from the empty spot where Hank's stall used to be, though he hadn't consciously chosen to sit there. The October wind was sharper now, carrying the smell of dead leaves and cold concrete. He was about to give up when a woman at a small nursery outside Philadelphia paused at his question.

"Fish Peppers?" she said slowly. "The striped ones? You're not talking about the Meadowlight Collective, are you?"

Jasper sat up straight. "The what?"

"It's a little intentional community about two hours west of here. Artists, growers, back-to-the-land types. They've been around since the seventies—started as a commune, evolved into something more structured. They're the only folks I know who still grow the old-stripe Fish Pepper varieties. Real purists about heirloom seeds." She paused. "Why? Is your pepper man there?"

"I think he might be," Jasper said, his heart pounding. "Can you give me an address?"

---

The drive took three hours, most of it on increasingly narrow roads that wound through the Pennsylvania countryside like they were trying to shake him off. Jasper's ancient Subaru—a car he'd bought for four hundred dollars from a guy in Bushwick and had spent more than that repairing—rattled over potholes and rumbled across a series of one-lane bridges. The GPS cheerfully insisted he'd arrived at his destination—a dirt driveway marked only by a hand-painted sign reading "Meadowlight" in faded blue letters, half-hidden by overgrown sumac.

The collective was a cluster of weathered buildings arranged around a central farmhouse—a Victorian that had been beautiful once and was now beautiful in a different way, the kind of beauty that comes from being used and loved rather than maintained. Gardens sprawled in every direction, some carefully tended and some allowed to grow wild and abundant, the beds still holding the last of the autumn harvest: kale gone slightly ragged, Brussels sprouts on thick stalks, the dried husks of squash vines. The air smelled of wood smoke and earth and something green and growing that seemed impossible for late October.

A few people worked in the fields, their postures unhurried, their movements deliberate. They barely looked up as Jasper parked. He got out and stood for a moment, taking in the silence—not the absence of sound, but a different kind of sound than he was used to. Wind in the trees. A rooster somewhere. The creak of an old building settling into its foundations.

He found a woman in her sixties sitting on the porch of the main house, shelling peas into a ceramic bowl balanced on her lap. She wore a heavy cardigan and work boots, and her silver hair was cut short in a way that suggested she'd done it herself.

"Hi," Jasper said, suddenly aware of how out of place he looked—Brooklyn skinny jeans and a vintage band t-shirt in the middle of rural Pennsylvania, his city energy vibrating against the stillness of the place like a tuning fork struck in a library. "I'm looking for someone. A guy who makes hot sauce. I think he might be here?"

The woman studied him for a long moment, her hands still moving through the peas with the automatic rhythm of someone who'd done this ten thousand times. "You a reporter?"

"No. Friend of a friend. He's been through some stuff, and I just... I need to talk to him."

"He's been through enough," she said, her voice protective but not hostile. "If you're here to cause more trouble—"

"I'm here to tell him he won," Jasper interrupted, surprising himself with the conviction in his voice. He sounded, for once, like a person who meant exactly what he said and nothing more. "We won. All of us. That's why I drove three hours to find him."

Another long pause. She looked at him the way you look at someone when you're deciding whether to trust them—not their words, but the thing behind the words. Then she nodded toward a greenhouse at the edge of the property, its glass panels catching the late afternoon light. "He's probably in there. With the peppers."

---

The greenhouse was humid and warm, a different world from the crisp October air outside. The smell hit Jasper first—rich, dense earth and green, living things, the specific scent of plants working hard to grow. Rows of pepper plants stretched toward the glass ceiling, their leaves broad and glossy, catching the filtered light in shades of green that ranged from almost-yellow to deep emerald. And among them, distinctive and unmistakable, were the Fish Peppers—their small fruits striped green and white, the pattern so vivid it looked almost painted.

At the far end of the greenhouse, a man was bent over a row of these plants, his hands moving with careful precision—pinching spent blossoms, checking the undersides of leaves, his touch as gentle as a parent's. He wore old jeans and a flannel shirt with the sleeves rolled up, and his forearms were dark with sun and soil.

"Hank?" Jasper called out.

The man straightened slowly. He was older than Jasper had expected—mid-fifties, maybe, with kind eyes and calloused hands and the slightly wary posture of someone who'd been surprised too many times by people who didn't mean well. But recognition flickered across his face, something softening.

"You're one of Emma's friends," he said. "From the market."

"Jasper," he confirmed, walking closer. The greenhouse was narrow, the path between the plant rows barely wide enough for one person. "Listen, I need to tell you something. About what happened after you left."

Hank set down his pruning shears on a wooden workbench. "How did you even find me?"

"Fish Peppers," Jasper said simply. "Emma remembered you talking about them. Took me about twenty phone calls, but..." He shrugged. "I'm persistent. It's one of my better qualities. Possibly my only one."

A small smile crossed Hank's face—the first one, Jasper guessed, that he'd worn in weeks. "She remembered that?"

"She remembered everything," Jasper said. "That's why I'm here."

He told Hank the whole story. All of it. He told him about the investigation that started as a drunken game over bad wine and deviled eggs, about Oliver's late night at the library, about the LLC discovery and Noah tracing it to the hospitality group. He told him about Emma visiting Dorothy and Sofia and learning the real shape of what had happened. He described the cease and desist letter—the legal threats, the handwritten note, the way Emma's voice had sounded on the phone when she called Olivia. He told him about the emergency dinner, the Thai food nobody ate, the fight between Noah and himself. And he told Hank what he'd done—how his phone call to Brenda had blown their cover, how his need to be the hero had put Emma in danger.

"I'm not going to pretend I'm not the reason it almost went wrong," Jasper said. "Because I am. But I'm also why I'm standing here right now. Because if I'm going to be the one who breaks things, I need to be the one who fixes them, too."

He described the swarm strategy—Oliver's talking points, Noah's anonymous infrastructure, Olivia's network, Emma's weaponized food industry grudges. He told Hank about the food critic, the investors pulling out, the momentum shifting. He didn't spare the details, and he didn't try to make himself look better than he was.

Hank listened without interrupting, his hands resting on the edge of the workbench, his expression unreadable. When Jasper finished, silence filled the greenhouse—the kind of silence that feels like it's holding its breath.

"They did all that?" Hank said finally. His voice was rough. "For me?"

"For your story," Jasper corrected gently. "Your great-grandmother's story. That's what they were trying to steal. Your shelf space, sure, but really? They wanted to erase you. Make it like you never existed." He gestured at the pepper plants, their striped fruits glowing in the afternoon light. "These plants, this history—they wanted to own it without earning it. And we couldn't let that happen."

Hank's hand rested on one of the striped leaves, his fingers gentle. "I don't know if I can go back," he said quietly. "Every time I think about setting up that stall again, about facing those people..."

"You don't have to decide right now," Jasper said. "But Emma's making dinner two weeks from Sunday. And I think you should meet the people who fought for you. Not because they wanted something, but because they cared about what your hot sauce meant." He paused. "That's all I'm asking. Just dinner."

Hank looked at the peppers, then at the dirt under his fingernails, then back at Jasper. "What if I'm not ready?"

"Then you're not ready," Jasper said simply. "But you'll never know unless you show up."

A long moment passed. The greenhouse hummed with the quiet energy of living things. Then Hank nodded slowly. "Two weeks?"

"Two weeks," Jasper confirmed. "I'll drive you back myself if you want."

"I'll drive myself," Hank said, and there was something firmer in his voice now—not bravado, but the quiet determination of a man choosing to stop running. "I need to do this part on my own." He extended his hand. "Thank you for finding me."

Jasper shook it, feeling the calluses, the strength, the soil still on Hank's palms. "Thank you for coming back."

As Jasper drove back toward Brooklyn that evening, the sun setting behind him and the highway stretching east into the gathering dark, his phone exploded with messages from the group chat. The story had gone viral. The mogul's investors were pulling out. The food critic's post had been picked up by national outlets.

But for Jasper, the real victory was smaller and more personal. He'd found Hank. He'd told the truth about his own mistakes. And he'd extended an invitation that mattered more than any anonymous email or viral post.

For once, his chaos had pointed in the right direction.
