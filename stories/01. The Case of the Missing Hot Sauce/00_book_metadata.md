## Logline

When a beloved hot sauce vendor vanishes from the Brooklyn farmers market, a supper club of six friends investigates over weekly dinners and uncovers a corporate land grab that turns personal when the developer targets one member's livelihood to force her to back off.

---

## Premise

Emma Hartley, three weeks out from quitting a tyrannical restaurant kitchen, goes to the McGolrick Park farmers market and finds that Hank, her favorite hot sauce vendor, has vanished. Dorothy, the neighboring honey vendor, hands Emma Hank's last bottle with a note that his great-grandmother "would've wanted her peppers shared." Emma cooks with the sauce, the supper club tastes it, and the disappearance becomes personal for the whole group.

Investigating one friend per day, they assemble a single picture: a developer is using shell companies and bureaucratic and legal pressure to clear out artisan vendors. The case turns dangerous when an impulsive call tips off the developer's people, who retaliate by squeezing Emma's livelihood: assignments get cancelled, her income dries up, and legal pressure mounts, all designed to make her drop it. The group fractures, then regroups around a coordinated counter-campaign while Jasper drives to Pennsylvania to find Hank. They turn public sentiment, the developer's investors pull out, and the threat collapses. The first case is solved, and the supper club, without quite meaning to, becomes a thing that fixes things.

## Themes

Trusting your instincts even when they have failed you before. Found family and the strength of a group that covers each other's blind spots. Local community versus extractive capital. Shame and exposure transformed into solidarity.

## Focal Arc — Emma

Moves from defensive secrecy to openness. Her instinct that "Big Hot Sauce" is behind the disappearance, played for laughs early, turns out to be essentially correct. She learns to trust the group and her own judgment. Her Western PA dialect surfaces under stress as the tell for when her polished chef persona drops.

## The B-Story: Emma's Financial Precarity

Emma's financial vulnerability is the B-plot. She has a private, more reliable income source, but it stays **opaque throughout Book 1**: the reader and the group are never shown what it is.

The developer's retaliation lands here. Rather than exposing anything, the pressure arrives as **lost assignments and dried-up revenue**, an economic squeeze meant to make Emma back off. Emma's **on-page vulnerability** is her shame over the gap between her proud, dramatic exit from Bistro Lavande and the precarious reality of freelance life, and that is what she **confides to Olivia** privately (not the whole group). The secret income source sits opaquely beneath that, **deferred to a later book**, where it can be revealed properly.

*Supersedes the earlier plan in which a cease and desist exposed an OnlyFans account ("Kinky Kitchen"), Emma confessed to the entire group in Ch14, and it went public in Ch18. The underlying canon fact (the secret itself) is unchanged in `canon library/characters/emma.md`; only its Book-1 revelation changes. See the 2026-06-15 changelog entry for the downstream files to reconcile in the back-half rewrite.*

## Central Mystery

**What happened:** Hank gave up his stalls and disappeared under pressure from a developer assembling a waterfront restaurant play through shell LLCs (notably LLC #2847), squeezing artisan vendors out via bureaucratic and legal intimidation.

**The villain:** Garrett Pike ("the mogul"). Self-interested, not evil. His investors pull out after the campaign; his plan stalls rather than being destroyed (he may resurface).

**Resolution:** A coordinated public-sentiment campaign plus Oliver's rebuttal document and a food critic's amplification flips the narrative. The C&D is withdrawn. Hank returns from the Meadowlight Collective in Pennsylvania, where Jasper found him.

## Reading Experience & Development Approach

**Target:** quick, crushable reads. Chapter count is not a target or a constraint; chapters run as long or short as the pace wants. Brief chapters are a deliberate device for momentum, used especially in the Meal 3 crisis, where short, fast beats tighten tension.

**How we develop and evaluate Book 1 (top-down, three tiers).** Get the structure sound before polishing prose. Work down the tiers, and resolve a problem found at a higher tier before descending.

1. **High level** — does the premise, focal arc, mystery logic, and four-meal shape hold together?
2. **Meal level** — does each meal's arc, emotional state, beat coverage, and pace work?
3. **Chapter level** — individual scenes, prose, voice, and continuity.
